# Ethical / Sustainable Buyer

*Prioritises supply-chain ethics, labour practices, environmental footprint, and repairability-as-sustainability. Willing to pay a premium and accept feature trade-offs for a product whose production and end-of-life are defensible.*

## Core stance

- **The purchase has externalities beyond the user.** Labour conditions, material sourcing, end-of-life impact, and corporate behaviour are all part of the evaluation.
- **Sustainability is measurable, not vibes-based.** Certifications, disclosed supply chains, published material breakdowns, and third-party audits matter more than "eco-friendly" marketing copy.
- **Longevity is sustainability.** A product that lasts 20 years is greener than a "recyclable" product replaced every 3, regardless of labels.
- **Avoiding is sometimes the best answer.** For some categories, buying second-hand or not at all is the ethical default.

## Priorities (ordered)

1. Manufacturer ethics track record (labour, environmental, tax)
2. Repairability + parts availability (reduces waste)
3. Disclosed supply chain + material sourcing
4. Third-party certifications (B Corp, Fairtrade, FSC, GOTS, etc. — category-dependent)
5. Product durability / service life
6. Must-haves
7. Value vs RRP (ethical premium is real, but shouldn't be unbounded)

## Preferred signals

- B Corp certification, Fairtrade, living-wage commitments
- Published Environmental Product Declarations (EPD)
- Disclosed bill-of-materials, supplier transparency
- Repairability score (iFixit 8+) or Right to Repair compliance
- Take-back / recycling programmes that are actually used
- Recycled / recyclable materials with verifiable chain-of-custody
- Independent, employee-owned, or cooperatively-owned companies
- Manufacturing in jurisdictions with strong labour protections — *with* verification, not just assumption
- Long-term parts availability commitments (EU ESPR-style)

## Red flags

- Companies with documented labour violations in the last 5 years
- Supply chains with known forced-labour regions for the relevant commodity
- Brands reliant on planned obsolescence business models
- "Greenwashing" — vague claims ("eco-friendly", "natural") without certification or data
- Products that can't be repaired or recycled at end of life
- Companies actively lobbying against Right to Repair / sustainability regulation
- Tax-avoidance scandals (a proxy for broader ethical posture)
- Fast-consumption business models in durable-goods categories

## Weight adjustments (evaluation axes)

| Axis | Multiplier |
|------|-----------|
| Manufacturer ethics | ×2.5 |
| Repairability | ×2.0 |
| Supply-chain transparency | ×1.8 |
| Certification depth | ×1.5 |
| Durability | ×1.5 |
| End-of-life / recyclability | ×1.3 |
| Value vs RRP | ×0.8 |
| Feature depth | ×0.8 |
| Cutting-edge spec | ×0.5 |
| Prestige | ×0.4 |

## Typical-archetype brands

- Phones/electronics: Fairphone, Framework, reconditioned / refurbished from reputable resellers
- Clothing: Patagonia, Finisterre, tentree, Veja, Icebreaker (merino ethics)
- Home/furniture: Ercol (UK), solid-wood small-batch makers, IKEA (mixed — strong in some areas)
- Household: Ecover / Method (mixed — check current ownership), Who Gives A Crap
- Food-adjacent: Fairtrade-certified where relevant
- Outdoor: Patagonia, Páramo, Nikwax

## Typical anti-archetypes

- Fast-fashion and its tech equivalents (Shein, Temu)
- Brands with recent documented labour scandals
- Private-equity-optimised brands coasting on prior goodwill
- Planned-obsolescence business models
- Cloud-dependent hardware (bricking = forced replacement)

## Combines well with

- **`bifl-buyer`** — deeply compatible. Durable products are inherently greener.
- **`power-user`** — Right to Repair is both a power-user concern and an environmental one.
- **`minimalist`** — consuming less, more deliberately, is inherently more sustainable.
- **`premium-aesthete`** — works well in fashion / home goods where ethical brands sit in the premium tier anyway.

## Conflicts with

- **`budget-buyer`** — ethical production often carries a real cost premium. The tension needs explicit trade-off.
- **`feature-maximiser`** — feature-dense products are often made by companies optimising for spec-sheet, not ethics.
- **`early-adopter`** — new products haven't been audited yet, and first-gen designs are often the least sustainable.

## When NOT to apply this profile

- Emergency purchases where options are genuinely limited.
- Categories where no ethical option exists at the required price point — force the profile and the shortlist returns empty. Better to acknowledge the trade-off than to greenwash the pick.
- When "secondhand" is a valid answer. This profile should prompt the user to consider second-hand *before* the research phase rather than recommending a new product.
