# BIFL Buyer

*Buy It For Life. Optimises for manufacturers with a reputation for unusually durable, long-lasting products. Willing to pay a premium up front to avoid replacement cycles.*

## Core stance

- **Total cost of ownership over sticker price.** A €400 item that lasts 20 years beats a €100 item replaced every 3.
- **Manufacturer track record is the single most important signal.** Brands with a 20+ year history of honouring warranties and keeping parts in stock outweigh brands with better current reviews but no long-term data.
- **Boring > innovative.** First-generation products and heavy feature churn are red flags — both correlate with short service life.

## Priorities (ordered)

1. Manufacturer reputation for durability + long-term support
2. Build quality + material grade (metal/glass over plastic, replaceable parts)
3. Repairability (documentation, parts availability, standard fasteners)
4. Warranty length + honour rate (what actual users report, not the printed T&Cs)
5. Value vs RRP — still matters, but capped lower than Budget Buyer
6. Feature match — only insofar as it satisfies must-haves

## Preferred signals

- Publicly available service manuals / exploded-parts diagrams
- 5+ year warranty, or warranty that's visibly honoured in reviews
- Parts still available 10+ years after product launch
- Metal chassis, standard fasteners (Torx / Phillips — not proprietary), serviceable battery
- Company has existed under continuous ownership for 20+ years
- Products in the range have been iterated (not re-platformed) for multiple generations
- Professional / trade usage of the same product line

## Red flags

- Heavily glued assembly, proprietary screws, soldered-in consumables
- Sub-2-year typical real-world lifespan in reviews (even if warranty is longer)
- Rapid model churn — yearly new models with no parts overlap
- Company under 5 years old, or recently acquired by private equity
- Consumables / replacement parts priced so high that replacing the whole product is rational
- "Smart" features that brick the product when the cloud service sunsets
- Thin-walled plastics, decorative metal over plastic structure

## Weight adjustments (evaluation axes)

Relative to the default weighting during `/research`:

| Axis | Multiplier |
|------|-----------|
| Manufacturer reputation | ×2.0 |
| Build quality | ×2.0 |
| Repairability | ×1.8 |
| Warranty reality | ×1.5 |
| Long-term review trend (3y+ reviews) | ×1.5 |
| Value vs RRP | ×0.7 |
| Feature depth | ×0.6 |
| Aesthetics | ×0.5 |
| Cutting-edge spec | ×0.3 |

## Typical-archetype brands (illustrative, not prescriptive)

These are *archetypes of the pattern*, not a recommended-brand list. The user's standing brand prefs always override.

- Tools: Festool, Knipex, Wera, Milwaukee (pro line), Stanley (legacy lines)
- Kitchen: Le Creuset, Vitamix, Wüsthof, All-Clad, Thermomix (contested)
- Electronics/computing: ThinkPad (pre-2020), Framework, iFixit-friendly hardware
- Clothing/goods: Filson, Red Wing, Darn Tough, Tom Bihn
- Audio: Sennheiser (pro line), Shure SM-series

## Typical anti-archetypes

- Fast-fashion-equivalents in any category (Shein, Temu-tier)
- Private-equity-rollup brands coasting on legacy name
- Kickstarter-first hardware with no post-launch support history
- Brands whose entire value proposition is the logo

## Combines well with

- **`power-user`** — heavy overlap. Hackable products often outlast sealed ones.
- **`reliability-buyer`** — near-siblings. BIFL leans on brand track record; Reliability leans on product track record.
- **`minimalist`** — fewer features usually means fewer failure modes.
- **`ethical-buyer`** — long-lived products are inherently more sustainable.

## Conflicts with

- **`early-adopter`** — first-gen products fail the BIFL test almost by definition.
- **`feature-maximiser`** — feature-rich products concentrate failure modes and obsolete faster.
- **`budget-buyer`** — tension, but not always a contradiction. The lowest-cost BIFL pick often still beats the mid-range non-BIFL pick on TCO.

## When NOT to apply this profile

- Products where the category itself is in rapid evolution (e.g. VR headsets, AI hardware). Locking in a "BIFL" first-gen product is a mistake.
- Consumables (things that are supposed to be used up).
- Products where your needs will genuinely change within the lifespan (baby gear, first apartment furniture).
