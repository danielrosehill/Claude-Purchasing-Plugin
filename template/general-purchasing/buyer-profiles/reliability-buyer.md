# Reliability Buyer

*Wants dependable, proven, unexciting products. Not looking for the most glamorous, highest-spec, or newest option. Wants the thing that works, every time, without drama.*

## Core stance

- **Proven > promising.** A 3-year-old product with a clean track record beats a new product with better specs and unknown reliability.
- **Boring is a feature.** Anything described as "revolutionary" or "industry-first" is a red flag.
- **The Toyota Corolla philosophy.** Not the fastest, prettiest, or cheapest — the one that starts every morning.
- **Mature ecosystems matter.** Established product lines with years of community troubleshooting beat novel products with no knowledge base.

## Priorities (ordered)

1. Long-term reliability track record (3y+ reviews, not launch reviews)
2. Mature product line — ideally the 3rd+ iteration of the same architecture
3. Manufacturer track record on *this specific line* (not just the brand broadly)
4. Service network + parts availability
5. Must-haves
6. Value — matters, but reliability premium is worth paying

## Preferred signals

- Product has been on sale for 2+ years with consistent reviews
- Heavy usage in professional / commercial settings (if pros run it, it works)
- Owner reports of 5+ years without major issues
- Mature, patched firmware — not first-generation anything
- Conservative engineering choices (proven components, standard interfaces)
- Strong community / owner forum with mundane, solvable issues rather than catastrophic ones
- Manufacturer has iterated the product line for multiple generations without re-platforming

## Red flags

- "New for this year" / "completely redesigned" / "next-generation"
- First-generation products in any category
- Heavy marketing around a single differentiating feature
- Vocal fanbase masking quality problems (hype can hide defects)
- Recent manufacturer ownership change
- Product categories experiencing rapid tech transition (wait one generation)
- Cloud dependencies that can disable the product retroactively

## Weight adjustments (evaluation axes)

| Axis | Multiplier |
|------|-----------|
| Long-term reliability (3y+ reviews) | ×2.5 |
| Product line maturity | ×2.0 |
| Service network quality | ×1.5 |
| Manufacturer track record on this line | ×1.5 |
| Warranty | ×1.2 |
| Value vs RRP | ×1.0 |
| Feature depth | ×0.7 |
| Aesthetics | ×0.6 |
| Cutting-edge spec | ×0.3 |
| "Innovation" marketing | ×0.1 |

## Typical-archetype brands

- Cars: Toyota, Honda, Mazda (non-turbo models)
- Appliances: Miele, Bosch (mature lines), Speed Queen (laundry)
- Computing: business-line ThinkPads, Dell Latitude, HP EliteBook
- Audio: mid-range Denon, Yamaha AV receivers (mature line)
- Tools: mid-tier Makita, Bosch Professional, DeWalt (pro line)

## Typical anti-archetypes

- Kickstarter / Indiegogo hardware
- Tesla-style "we'll fix it in software" products
- Startup brands under 5 years old
- Products on their first major redesign cycle
- Anything pitched as "disrupting" the category

## Combines well with

- **`bifl-buyer`** — near-siblings. BIFL leans on brand, Reliability leans on product-line track record.
- **`budget-buyer`** — "cheap and boring that works" is a strong default for utility purchases.
- **`minimalist`** — fewer features = fewer things to break.
- **`ethical-buyer`** — reliable products get replaced less often.

## Conflicts with

- **`early-adopter`** — direct contradiction.
- **`feature-maximiser`** — more features = more surface area for problems.
- **`premium-aesthete`** — prestige lines often chase design over proven engineering.

## When NOT to apply this profile

- Categories where "proven" means "stagnant" — e.g. most consumer tech where a 3-year-old product is functionally obsolete.
- When the user's need is rare / specialised and mainstream-reliable options don't cover it.
- Entertainment / hobby purchases where the fun is partly in the newness.
