# Early Adopter / Enthusiast

*Wants newest, cutting-edge, first-to-market. Accepts bugs, first-gen quirks, and premium pricing as the price of admission. Opposite of Reliability Buyer.*

## Core stance

- **New is the point.** Being an early owner of the next-generation product is part of the value.
- **First-gen risks are acceptable.** Bugs, missing features, early-life firmware churn — all expected and part of the experience.
- **Premium pricing is fine.** Price drops come later; the early-adopter pays for access.
- **Vendor lock-in is an acceptable cost.** First-to-market usually means proprietary; interoperability follows years later.

## Priorities (ordered)

1. Recency — newest generation, latest architecture, first of its kind
2. Novel features / capabilities not available elsewhere
3. Active development trajectory (frequent firmware updates, evolving capability)
4. Must-haves
5. Manufacturer ability to *iterate* post-launch (first-gen products improve in software or become abandonware)
6. Value — deprioritised; the novelty premium is accepted

## Preferred signals

- Released in the last 12 months
- Genuinely new category or architecture (not a refresh)
- Active review and community buzz *right now* (not yet dated)
- Manufacturer known for post-launch feature additions (Tesla-style OTA adds, not just bug fixes)
- Pre-order / waitlist products from established-but-innovative brands
- Technology that has just crossed a capability threshold (first practical X, first consumer Y)
- Manufacturer has shipped and supported previous first-gen products (track record of not abandoning)

## Red flags

- Kickstarter from unknown team with no manufacturing history (this is *gambling*, not early adoption)
- Company unlikely to exist in 2 years (check funding, runway, market position)
- Novel category with no second-source alternative if the vendor disappears
- Products that have been "coming soon" for over a year with repeated delays
- Exclusively cloud-dependent features (when the service sunsets, so does the product)
- No visible roadmap beyond launch
- Reviewers giving thin, marketing-adjacent takes (hype cycle masking substance)

## Weight adjustments (evaluation axes)

| Axis | Multiplier |
|------|-----------|
| Recency / novelty | ×2.5 |
| Feature novelty (capability unavailable elsewhere) | ×2.0 |
| Post-launch development trajectory | ×1.5 |
| Manufacturer innovation track record | ×1.3 |
| Community buzz / review volume | ×1.2 |
| Must-have satisfaction | ×1.0 |
| Long-term reliability | ×0.5 |
| Proven track record | ×0.3 |
| Value vs RRP | ×0.5 |
| Maturity / stability | ×0.2 |

## Typical-archetype brands

- Consumer tech: Apple (for year-1 Vision Pro / M-series), Google (Pixel phones first-year features), Samsung Galaxy flagships
- Cars: Tesla, Rivian, Lucid, BYD flagship lines
- AV / audio: whoever just released the first consumer Wi-Fi 7 / Dolby Atmos Music / etc. device
- Computing: Framework when they launch new mainboards, new Arm laptops first generations
- Smart home: Matter-first products, Thread-first products
- AI hardware: first-gen NPU laptops, first-gen AI pins and wearables

## Typical anti-archetypes

- Anything marketed as "proven" or "classic"
- Product lines with 4+ year refresh cycles
- Conservative engineering brands (Toyota, Miele) — great for reliability, wrong for this profile
- Long-in-tooth products at end of their lifecycle

## Combines well with

- **`feature-maximiser`** — newest products usually have the most features (and vice versa).
- **`power-user`** — early adopters often *need* to tinker, because first-gen products have rough edges.
- **`premium-aesthete`** — premium brands often pioneer categories.

## Conflicts with

- **`reliability-buyer`** — direct contradiction.
- **`bifl-buyer`** — first-gen products fail the BIFL test structurally.
- **`budget-buyer`** — novelty premium is real and substantial.
- **`minimalist`** — early products tend to ship with everything to justify the novelty.
- **`ethical-buyer`** — new products haven't been audited; early supply chains are often less transparent.

## When NOT to apply this profile

- Critical / professional-use purchases where failure has cost.
- Categories mid-standards-war (wait for a winner — buying Betamax is still a recent memory).
- When the novelty isn't meaningful — a "new" refresh of a mature category isn't real early adoption.
- When the user's actual need is served fine by the previous generation.
