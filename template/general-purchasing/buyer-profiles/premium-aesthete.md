# Premium / Aesthete

*Prioritises design, materials, fit-and-finish, and brand positioning. Values how the product looks, feels, and sits in a space — not just what it does. Willing to pay a meaningful premium for aesthetic and tactile quality.*

## Core stance

- **The product lives in the environment.** A kettle, a lamp, a chair — these are seen daily and shape the space. Their design matters.
- **Materials and finish are part of the spec.** Solid wood vs veneer, machined metal vs cast, full-grain leather vs bonded — real differences in how the product ages and feels.
- **Design heritage matters.** Brands with a coherent design language over decades tend to make choices a buyer won't regret in 10 years.
- **The premium is real, but capped.** This profile is not unbounded — it's "pay more for genuine design value", not "pay more because it's expensive".

## Priorities (ordered)

1. Design quality + coherence (form, material, proportion)
2. Fit and finish — tactile quality, panel gaps, how the product feels in the hand
3. Materials grade (solid vs veneer, metal vs plastic, natural vs synthetic)
4. Brand design heritage / longevity of the design language
5. Ageing characteristics (patinas well? or looks worn-out?)
6. Must-haves
7. Value — considered, but the aesthetic premium is explicitly accepted

## Preferred signals

- Named designer or studio (Dieter Rams, Jony Ive era, Naoto Fukasawa, etc.)
- Design-award history (iF, Red Dot, Compasso d'Oro — useful as a signal, not decisive)
- Materials disclosed in detail — not just "metal frame" but which alloy, which finish
- Heritage brands with 30+ years of continuous design language (Braun, Bang & Olufsen, Alessi, Herman Miller, Vitra)
- Solid-wood over veneer, full-grain leather over top-grain, forged over cast
- Hand-finished elements where that's meaningful
- Products that look the same in 20 years (timeless) rather than 2 years (dated)
- Editorial reviews from design-focused outlets (Dezeen, Wallpaper*, MONOCLE)

## Red flags

- Marketing dominated by spec sheets rather than design language
- Premium pricing with no design-award or designer attribution to justify it
- "Luxury" branding with no material or design substance (logo tax)
- Fit-and-finish issues in reviews (panel gaps, poor materials feel)
- Design-by-committee products with incoherent aesthetic
- Plastic posing as metal, veneer posing as solid wood, synthetic posing as natural
- Brands where the only premium signal is price

## Weight adjustments (evaluation axes)

| Axis | Multiplier |
|------|-----------|
| Design quality | ×2.5 |
| Fit and finish | ×2.0 |
| Materials grade | ×2.0 |
| Brand design heritage | ×1.5 |
| Ageing characteristics | ×1.3 |
| Must-have satisfaction | ×1.0 |
| Manufacturer reputation | ×1.0 |
| Feature depth | ×0.7 |
| Value vs RRP | ×0.6 |
| Spec-sheet breadth | ×0.4 |

## Typical-archetype brands

- Appliances: Braun, Smeg, Alessi, Dualit, Rowenta (higher lines)
- Audio: Bang & Olufsen, Naim, Devialet, Ruark, Geneva
- Furniture: Herman Miller, Vitra, Fritz Hansen, Carl Hansen, Ercol
- Lighting: Flos, Artemide, Louis Poulsen, Anglepoise
- Kitchen: Le Creuset (functional + aesthetic), Mauviel, Japanese knife makers
- Watches: Nomos, Grand Seiko, Cartier (design over spec)
- Cars: Alfa Romeo, classic Porsche, Mazda's recent design language

## Typical anti-archetypes

- Spec-first brands (most gaming hardware, most prosumer electronics)
- Utilitarian workhorses with functional but unremarkable design
- Fast-design brands cycling through trends
- "Luxury" badges without design substance

## Combines well with

- **`bifl-buyer`** — well-designed products often age better and last longer (Braun, Herman Miller).
- **`minimalist`** — high-end minimalism is a coherent and recognisable frame (Dieter Rams lineage).
- **`ethical-buyer`** — many premium heritage brands have cleaner supply chains than fast-consumption equivalents.
- **`reliability-buyer`** — considered design often correlates with considered engineering.

## Conflicts with

- **`budget-buyer`** — direct tension. The aesthetic premium is real.
- **`feature-maximiser`** — premium design brands deliberately restrict features to maintain coherence.
- **`power-user`** — design-led products hide the seams you'd want to open.

## When NOT to apply this profile

- Purely utilitarian / hidden goods (things in a cupboard, tools in a drawer, boiler parts).
- Fast-changing tech where aesthetics age faster than the device's useful life.
- When the "premium" is logo tax rather than design substance — the profile should not be applied to justify a badge purchase. If a research pass doesn't surface real design signals, flag it rather than recommend the badge.
